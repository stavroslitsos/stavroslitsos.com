Professional

Manual therapist, chiropractor, osteopath or naprapath – what is the difference?

If you have back, neck or shoulder pain, it is not always obvious who to see. Four professions work with musculoskeletal problems and use partly similar methods – but their education, their rights within the health service and their approach differ.

All four are authorised health personnel

This is news to many: since 1 May 2022, osteopaths and naprapaths are also authorised health personnel in Norway. Physiotherapists, manual therapists and chiropractors were already authorised.

Authorisation means the professional title is protected, that the practitioner is covered by the Health Personnel Act and the Patients' Rights Act, that records are kept to statutory standards, and that you are covered by the Norwegian System of Patient Injury Compensation. The key distinction today is therefore not whether a practitioner is authorised, but their educational background and what rights they hold within the public health system.

Manual therapist

A manual therapist is a physiotherapist with an additional two-year master's degree in manual therapy (in Norway at the University of Bergen). The education places strong emphasis on clinical examination and differential diagnosis – identifying what is actually causing the problem, and recognising conditions that belong with a physician.

Since 2006, manual therapists have held extended rights:

  • Primary contact – you can come directly, without a doctor's referral
  • Refer for imaging (X-ray, MRI, CT) covered by the national insurance scheme
  • Refer on to specialist health services
  • Issue sick leave for up to 12 weeks
  • Reimbursement through HELFO where the practitioner holds an operating agreement

Treatment combines manual techniques – joint mobilisation, manipulation and soft-tissue work – with tailored exercise and rehabilitation.

Chiropractor

Chiropractors complete a five-year education, often taken abroad, focused on examination and treatment of joints and the spine, with manipulation as a central method. Chiropractors hold the same extended rights as manual therapists: primary contact, referral for imaging and to specialists, sick leave for up to 12 weeks and reimbursement. In practice the two professions overlap considerably – the difference lies more in educational route and professional tradition than in which complaints they treat.

Osteopath

Osteopaths hold a four-year bachelor's degree and take a holistic approach in which the body is seen as a whole. Treatment includes soft-tissue techniques, joint mobilisation and manipulation. Osteopaths became authorised health personnel in 2022, but currently do not have the right to issue sick leave, to refer to specialist health services, or to request imaging at the national insurance scheme's expense. Treatment is normally not covered by the national insurance scheme.

Naprapath

Naprapaths complete a four-year education, mainly in Sweden, focused on the neuromuscular system – the interplay between muscles, joints and nerves. Treatment combines soft-tissue work, joint manipulation and exercise. Like osteopaths, naprapaths were authorised in 2022 and likewise do not hold sick-leave or referral rights.

What does this mean for you?

All four professions can help with common musculoskeletal complaints, and many patients do well whoever they see.

The difference matters most when the problem is unclear, complex or long-lasting. Then it has practical consequences whether your practitioner can request an MRI, refer you on to an orthopaedic or rheumatology specialist and issue sick leave – without an extra detour via your GP. That saves time, and the right treatment can start sooner. My advice is to choose based on your complaint, not on job title alone.
